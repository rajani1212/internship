Ideation

Let us take two products as examples for understanding it’s ideation and conceptualization.

Product 1 – Google Assistant

Google Assistant’s arrival in 2016 stemmed from a desire to improve upon existing virtual assistants and address their limitations.

Drawbacks of previous Assistants:

  • Limited Understanding: Early assistants often misinterpreted commands or struggled with complex requests. For example, an assistant might not understand the difference between “play music” and “play my playlist”.
  • Reliance of Keywords: Users had to remember specific phrases or keywords to trigger actions. This made interactions less intuitive and natural.
  • Lack of Context: Without context, assistants might provide irrelevant response. For example, if a user asked for “directions to work” it wouldn’t consider your current location or preferred mode of transportation.
  • Limited Integration: Early assistants often functioned in silos, unable to access information from other apps or services.

By addressing these limitations, Google Assistant aimed to create a more user-friendly and versatile experience. It focused on understanding natural language, learning user context, and leveraging the power of Google’s vast data and ecosystem.

Reasons for Google Assistant:

  • Focus on Natural Language Understanding (NLU): Previous assistants often required specific keywords or phrases commands to function. Google Assistant aimed to understand natural language, allowing users to speak in a more conversational way.
  • Contextual Awareness: Early assistants struggled to understand the context of a conversation. Google Assistant was designed to learn from past interactions and preferences, personalizing responses and offering more relevant suggestions.
  • Integration with Google Services: Many assistants lacked deep integration with other platforms. Google Assistant aimed to leverage Google’s suite of services like Search, Maps, and Calendar providing a more unified user experience.
  • Mobile-first Approach: As smartphones became ubiquitous, Google Assistant prioritized working seamlessly on mobile devices, making it readily available on the go.

Product 2 – Google Pay

The idea behind Google Pay (Formerly Android Pay) to address the limitations of traditional payment methods and existing mobiles wallets.

Drawbacks of Previous Payment Methods:

  • Physical Cards: Carrying and managing multiple cards can be cumbersome and there’s a risk of theft or loss.
  • Limited Mobile Wallets (Like carrier-specific solutions): These wallets might have restricted functionality, limited compatibility with stores, or lack integration with other services.
  • Security Concerns: Swiping physical cards or using outdated mobile wallets might expose users to fraud if security features are weak.
  • Friction at checkout: Entering PINs or manually launching separate wallet apps could slow down the checkout process.

Motivations for Google Pay:

  • Improved Convenience: Google Pay streamlines the in-store checkout process. Users can make contactless payment with their smartphones eliminating the need to carry physical cards.
  • Enhanced Security: Google Pay prioritizes security by tokenization. Instead of sharing actual card details with merchants, a unique virtual account number is used for each transaction.
  • Wider Compatibility: With Google Pay, Users don’t need specific NFC-enabled phones. It leverages Host card emulation (HCE) technology, allowing payments on many existing android smartphones.
  • Unified Experience: Google Pay integrates with various Google services like Gmail, Maps and loyalty programs. It aims to be a one-stop shop for managing payments and digital wallets.

Let's take Google Chrome as an example to explain product maintenance.

  • Bug Fixes:

    • Google Chrome regularly releases updates to fix bugs. For instance, if users report that the browser crashes when they visit certain websites, the development team investigates and resolves these issues in subsequent updates.

  • Updates and Upgrades:

    • Chrome is known for its frequent updates, which include new features and improvements. For example, Google might add new developer tools, user interface enhancements, or integrate new technologies like WebAssembly to improve performance and capabilities.

  • Performance Optimization:

    • The Chrome team continuously works on optimizing the browser's speed and efficiency. This includes reducing memory usage, improving load times, and making the browser more responsive. For instance, they may introduce a new JavaScript engine or refine the browser's resource management.

  • Security Updates:

    • Security is a critical aspect of any web browser. Google regularly releases security patches to protect users from vulnerabilities like zero-day exploits, phishing attacks, and malware. They also participate in bug bounty programs to encourage external security researchers to find and report vulnerabilities.

  • Support and Documentation:

    • Google provides extensive support and documentation for Chrome users. This includes a help center with articles and troubleshooting guides, as well as community forums where users can ask questions and share solutions.

  • Compliance and Compatibility:

    • Chrome must stay compliant with new web standards and regulations, such as GDPR for data protection. Additionally, it ensures compatibility with a wide range of websites and web applications by adhering to updated web standards and protocols. By continuously maintaining Chrome through these activities, Google ensures that the browser remains secure, fast, and feature-rich, providing a reliable and satisfying experience for millions of users worldwide.


Deploying Google Workspace:

  • Planning and Preparation:

    • Google and the client organization plan the deployment, including timelines and required resources. They assess the existing IT infrastructure and prepare for data migration from old systems.

  • Installation:

    • Google Workspace apps (like Gmail, Drive, Docs) are installed and configured on user devices. This includes setting up accounts, permissions, and access controls.

  • Configuration:

    • The deployment team configures the Workspace settings according to the organization's requirements, such as setting up email domains, security settings, and compliance policies.

  • Testing:

    • The system is tested to ensure all services are working correctly. This might include pilot testing with a small group of users to identify any issues before a full-scale rollout.

  • Training and Documentation:

    • Google provides training sessions and materials for employees to learn how to use the new tools. Comprehensive documentation is made available for reference.

  • Go-Live:

    • The organization officially switches to Google Workspace. All users start using the new system for their daily tasks.

  • Post-Deployment Support:

    • Google and the organization's IT team provide ongoing support to address any issues and ensure smooth operation. Feedback is collected for continuous improvement and future updates.
